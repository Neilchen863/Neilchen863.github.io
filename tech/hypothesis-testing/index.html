Hypothesis Testing

Outline

  1. Assumptions
  2. Stating hypothesis
  3. Test statistics and its null distribution
  4. P-value and its intepretation
  5. Conclusion

Step1: Assumptions

For proportion

  1. The variable measured is categorical.
  2. The data are obtained using randomization.
  3. The sample size n is sufficiently large that the sampling distribution of the sample proportion $\hat{p}$ is approximately Normal when the null is true.

(This will be true if n⋅p0⋅(1 − p0) ≥ 5, where p0 is the value specified in H0.

For mean

  1. The variable measured is quantitative.
  2. The data are obtained using randomization.
  3. The population distribution is approximately Normal. This assumption is crucial when n is small.

Step2: Stating hypothesis

The null hypothesis of a test about the mean has the form:

H₀: μ = μ₀ where μ₀ is the hypothesized mean of the population.

A two-sided alternative hypothesis would be:

H₁: μ ≠ μ₀

A one-sided alternative hypothesis is either:

H₁: μ < μ₀ or H₁: μ > μ₀

Step3: Test statistics and its null distribution

For proportion

The test statistic of a test describes how far that point estimate falls from the parameter value given in the null hypothesis.

When H~0~ is true, p = p~0~, the sampling distribution of $\hat{p}$ is then:

$\hat{p}$ ~ N (p~0~, $\frac{p_0(1 - p_0)}{n}$)

The test statistic is the distance of $\hat{p}$ from p~0~ in terms of its standard deviation:

$$Z = \frac{\hat{p} - p_0}{\sqrt{\frac{p_0(1 - p_0)}{n}}}$$

This Z ~ N(0,1).

For mean

Test statistic:

$$T = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu_0}{\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}}$$ If H0 is true, then T follows a t distribution with (n − 1) degrees of freedom.

Step4: P-value and its intepretation

The p-value summaries the evidence against H0, and in support of H1.

Calculate p-value:

If H1 is a two sided test, then p-value is the two areas (left and right tail probabilities) of the test statistics Z .

If H1 is a right-sided test, then p-value is the area on the right side of test statistics Z .

If H1 is a left-sided test, then p-value is the area on the left of test statistics Z

Step5: Conclusion

We summarize the test by reporting and interpreting the p-value in the context of the study.

If it is small, we say we have strong evidence against H0.

If it is large (usually more than 0.2), we say we do not have strong evidence against H0.

If a significance level α is given, we compare p-value with α to conclude on reject or do not reject H0.
